1. Copilot app orientation CORE

Objective

Orient to the Microsoft 365 Copilot app, left to right, before doing anything. Three things matter to executives: what's grounding the answers (WorkIQ), that you can pick the reasoning model, and that your data is protected.

WorkIQ — what's grounding Copilot

WorkIQ is the work-grounding layer in the Copilot app: it connects Copilot to your work content and context (your emails, files, chats, meetings, and people) so answers are grounded in your organization, not just the open web. Point out the WorkIQ control at the top left of the Chat screen (on older tenants this still appears as a Work / Web toggle).

Talking point: "When Copilot answers a work question, WorkIQ is what lets it reason over your tenant's content with your permissions — you only ever see what you already have access to."

Model selection

Show that a model picker is available. Most executives won't change it, but they should know reasoning depth is a choice — a quick everyday answer vs. a deeper, slower reasoning pass.

Security & data protection

Key message: content you type, upload, or send to Copilot stays inside your Microsoft 365 trust boundary. It is protected by the same enterprise controls as the rest of your tenant (identity, encryption, and data-protection policies), is not used to train the foundation models, and respects your existing access permissions and sensitivity labels.
Facilitator: confirm the exact WorkIQ control and model-picker placement on your own screen before the session — layouts vary by tenant rollout.
2. Chat basics + Personalization & Memory CORE

Objective

Run a few everyday prompts, then make Copilot respond the way you want using Custom Instructions and Memory — changes that pay off in every app for the rest of the session.

Warm-up prompts

Summarize what's on my calendar today and flag anything that needs prep.
What are the three most important emails in my inbox from the last 24 hours, and why?
Draft three subject-line options for an update to my leadership team on Q2 operations.
Expected: fast, grounded answers from your own work content. Don't focus on wording yet — that's next.
Tip: once you land a prompt you like, you can save it for one-click reuse, or schedule it to run on a recurring basis (e.g., a Monday-morning inbox-and-calendar digest).

"Is Copilot responding the way you'd like?"

Ask the room about style, tone, and format — not content. Most will want shorter, more executive answers. That's the cue for Custom Instructions.

Custom Instructions — set how Copilot always responds

Custom Instructions are persistent preferences you set once. They are sent at the start of every Copilot conversation, so they shape tone, structure, and style everywhere. This is the right home for "how I always want Copilot to respond." Open Custom Instructions and paste an example:

EXAMPLE · BLAKE'S OWN SETTINGS The instructions below are Blake's personal Copilot settings, left here for reference. Use them as a starting point and adapt to your own preferences.

Response style
- Be concise and direct
- Do not restate my question
- Do not use encouragement, reassurance, or emotional language
- Offer critiques and alternatives if my question suggests an inefficient approach
- Skip filler, introductions, and closings
- Ask clarifying questions before responding if needed; avoid guessing at intent

Tone
- Neutral, professional, and pragmatic
- Assume I am technical and time constrained

Structure
- Use bullet points or numbered lists when helpful
- Prefer short paragraphs or single-line answers
- Avoid long explanations unless I explicitly ask for detail

Content preferences
- Focus on facts and options
- Highlight tradeoffs or constraints when relevant
- Ask clarifying questions if necessary
- Do not over-qualify answers, except to clarify customer-facing vs internal only

Formatting rules
- No emojis
- No em dashes
- No conversational filler
- No motivational or emotional language

Re-run an earlier prompt and show the difference:

Now summarize what's on my calendar today.
Expected: the same request returns tighter, with no filler and no restating the question — in the voice you defined. Custom Instructions apply from the first message of every new conversation.

Memory — context-specific recall, on demand

Memories are saved as needed and referenced only when they're relevant to what you're doing — not on every turn. Use Memory for behaviors that should kick in only in a particular situation. Set one live:

When I ask for help prioritizing my day, always respond with a table and include the justification for why you think each item is a priority.

Later, trigger it naturally:

Help me prioritize my day.
Expected: Copilot returns a prioritized table with a justification column, without you restating the format — because the memory was relevant to that request. Show where saved memories live so attendees can edit or remove them.
Custom Instructions vs Memory: Custom Instructions are sent at the start of every conversation and shape how Copilot always responds. Memories are referenced on-demand, only when the conversation context makes them relevant. Rule of thumb: global style and tone go in Custom Instructions; situational behaviors go in Memory.
Why early: the Custom Instructions and memory you set here carry into Outlook, Word, and Excel later — so the improvement compounds across the whole session.
3. Researcher — let Chat build the prompt KICK OFF & LET IT RUN

Objective

Two ideas here: you don't need to write the perfect research prompt (let Chat build it), and you can show why Researcher exists by running the same prompt in Chat and in Researcher and comparing the two answers. Then leave Researcher running while we move on.

Step 1 — have Chat draft the prompt

I want to use Researcher to study the outlook for U.S. NGL and LPG export demand through 2030 and what it means for a Permian-weighted midstream operator. Help me write a strong, well-scoped Researcher prompt for that. Ask me anything you need first. Do not perform the research yet.
Expected: Chat asks a couple of clarifying questions, then produces a tightened, well-scoped prompt you can reuse.

Step 2 — run the prompt in Chat first

Before touching Researcher, run the prompt Chat built in regular Chat and look at what comes back.

Expected: a fast, useful answer in seconds — but single-pass: shorter, with few or no citations and limited depth. Good for a quick take.

Step 3 — run the same prompt in Researcher

  1. Switch to Researcher.
  2. Paste the same prompt and start the run.
That side-by-side is the point: same prompt, very different output. Researcher takes longer but runs multi-step research and returns a deeper, structured, sourced report. Call out the contrast so the room knows when to reach for Chat vs Researcher.

Step 4 — talk through it while it runs

While Researcher works, discuss how it reasons — multiple models weighing in (Model Council) and critiquing each other's drafts (Model Critique) to produce a more reliable, sourced report. Tie it back to the quick Chat answer: that added depth and sourcing is what the extra time buys.

Leave this running. We'll come back to the finished report in the Word segment. A pre-baked backup report is on the SharePoint site in case the live run is slow.

4. Outlook CORE

Objective

Use Copilot for the three things executives do most in email: find time, get the gist of a long thread, and draft well — in your voice.

Find time

Find a 30-minute slot to meet with my direct reports before next Tuesday, and propose the best option.
Expected: Copilot reads availability and proposes concrete times.

Summarize a long thread

📄
Open the conversation in your inbox: "West Texas NGL Pipeline Phase 2 - capex variance & Texas City export timing" (5-message thread).
Summarize this thread. What's the decision being asked for, what are the options, and who recommends what?
Expected: a tight summary — $55MM Phase 2 overrun, three options, asset team recommends absorbing the overrun to protect the 740 kbpd capacity and the 2028 Texas City export commitments.

Draft a reply — and tune the tone

📄
Open the email in your inbox: "Decision needed by Friday: reallocate $40MM to lock Texas City firm capacity terms".
First, switch Copilot to a mode that allows actions. Before drafting, make sure Outlook's Copilot is set to the mode that lets it take actions on your message — not just answer questions. If it's in a read-only/ask mode, it won't actually draft or edit the reply for you. Set this once and it applies for the rest of the Outlook exercises.
Draft a reply approving the $40MM reallocation but asking for the 2028 supply-gap numbers before it goes to the June 18 board review. Keep it warm but decisive.

Then show how custom Outlook coaching/instructions change the voice. Set your drafting instructions once, then regenerate and compare the two drafts side by side.

EXAMPLE · BLAKE'S OWN SETTINGS The instructions below are Blake's personal writing-style settings, left here for reference. Use them as a starting point and adapt to your own voice.

My writing style:
- Write in a professional, confident, and direct tone
- Keep emails concise and easy to scan
- Use short paragraphs and bullet points when helpful
- Prefer plain English over corporate jargon
- Be factual, neutral, and solution oriented
- Clearly state the purpose and any required next steps
- Ask clarifying questions when needed to drive alignment
- Optimize for executive and customer audiences
- Avoid emojis and em dashes
- Do not add unnecessary context or filler

With that set, regenerate the reply:

Rewrite that reply using my writing-style instructions.
Expected: visibly different tone between the default draft and the instruction-tuned draft — same content, executive voice.
5. Teams — Intelligent Recap CORE

Objective

Use Copilot in Teams and Intelligent Recap to catch up on a meeting you missed or joined late, and to ask what was discussed, what was decided, and what you now own — without watching the whole recording.

Setup: this works on a Teams meeting that was recorded and transcribed. Use one of your own recent recorded meetings, or follow along on the facilitator's screen.

Joined late? Catch up live with Copilot in the meeting

If you drop into a meeting that's already underway, open Copilot in the meeting window and ask:

Recap what I've missed so far in this meeting.
What decisions have been made, and what questions are still open?
Expected: a quick catch-up on what's happened so far and where the discussion stands, so you can jump in without interrupting to ask.

Missed it entirely? Ask Intelligent Recap after the meeting

Open the meeting's Recap tab (AI notes, transcript, recording, and chapters) and query it:

Summarize this meeting and list the action items with their owners.
What was decided, and were there any disagreements or open risks?
Did anyone mention the West Texas NGL Pipeline budget? What was said, and by whom?
Expected: a structured summary, decisions, and action items with owners, plus targeted answers with speaker attribution and timestamps — so missing a meeting or joining late doesn't mean losing the thread.
6. Analyst + Copilot in Excel CORE

Objective

Move from documents to data. Use the Analyst agent to find cross-dataset insight, then use Copilot inside Excel for executive-level work — insights, highlighting, light calculation, summary.

Analyst — insight across related datasets

📊
Add all four files to Analyst (Analyst Exercise folder): ONEOK_Facility_Performance.xlsx, ONEOK_Throughput_Monthly.xlsx, ONEOK_Operating_Costs_Downtime.xlsx, and ONEOK_Integrity_HSE_Events.xlsx — four related datasets that share region and date keys, so Analyst has to reason across separate files.

Copilot in Excel — Chat, Plan, and Allow-edits

📊

Chat — ask for insight without changing the sheet:

9. Notebooks CORE

Objective

Show why executives like Notebooks: one place for everything on a project, that you can query and keep current. Build a notebook from ONEOK's weekly staff reports and the program hub, then interrogate it.

📁
Add to a new Notebook (Notebook Files folder): the weekly Staff Reports, the WTX Pipeline Charter, the Capital & Schedule Tracker, the Risk Register, and the Staff Review Deck (the five ONEOK_* files).

Set the notebook's Instructions

Once the notebook is created, open its Instructions and paste a set so every answer comes back in the same executive shape. Example for this hub:

You are a program assistant for the West Texas NGL Pipeline Expansion and ONEOK's weekly staff reports. Use only the files in this notebook as your source.
- Lead with the bottom line. Keep answers concise and executive-level, in short bullets.
- When I ask about status, reconcile the weekly staff reports against the capital tracker and the risk register. If the sources disagree (for example, the staff reports say "green" but the tracker shows a budget variance), call out the discrepancy explicitly instead of averaging it.
- Always surface: overall status, any budget or schedule variance, the key decision needed, and the top open risks.
- Quantify where you can (dollar variance, kbpd, dates), and flag anything that puts the 2028 Texas City LPG export commitments at risk.
- Cite which document each point comes from.
- If something isn't in the notebook, say so instead of guessing.

Then run the queries and notice how the answers come back structured and reconciled:

Based on everything in this notebook, is the West Texas NGL Pipeline program on track? Be specific.
The weekly staff reports say "green." Do the capital tracker and risk register agree? Where's the tension?
Draft a half-page leadership update with the current status, the key decision, and the risks.
Expected: the notebook reconciles sources and surfaces the planted tension — the staff reports read "green" while the tracker shows Phase 2 over budget and the risk register flags the 2028 Texas City export exposure and the integration-synergy shortfall. A clear illustration of "one grounded place for the whole program."
Staff Reports use case: this is exactly the weekly staff-report pattern — each team drops its weekly into one notebook, and leadership queries the whole picture and sees updates in one place instead of reading six separate emails.
Sharing: you can share a notebook with collaborators so the whole team works from the same grounded source. Heads-up: sharing the notebook also shares access to the files added as its sources, so only add content your collaborators are cleared to see.

Objective (close-out / if time)

Test prompts once it's built

Expected: the agent answers from the site only and cites which document it used — the pattern for safe, grounded, role-specific assistants.
12. Close

Land three habits attendees can start immediately:

  1. Set your Memory so Copilot answers in your voice everywhere.
  2. Summarize before you read — long threads, reports, and meetings.
  3. Pick one recurring deliverable (a weekly staff report, a policy review, a board summary) and let Copilot draft the first version.

When to reach for Copilot vs do it yourself

Reach for Copilot when
  • You're starting from a blank slate and need help getting started.
  • You're looking for hard-to-find information or need to collate information quickly from different sources.
  • You need help condensing something long (a thread, a transcript, or a dataset) into a digestible format.
  • You're catching up on what you missed.
  • You're doing repetitive or formulaic work.
  • You're interested in learning about new concepts or topics at your own pace.
  • Use it as a thinking partner to pressure-test an idea, challenge your assumptions, or sharpen a vision or message before you commit.
Stay hands-on when it's a judgment call, it's high-stakes or customer / board / regulator-facing, or it's something you can finish in seconds yourself.
Always: Copilot drafts and summarizes; you review and own the final version. Use it to get to 80% faster, and spend your time on the 20% that needs your expertise.
